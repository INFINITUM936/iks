Chapter One
IKS – An Overview
Q1What is the current status of IKS in India? Briefly explain the reasons for its current status.

IKS (Indian Knowledge System) is slowly coming back to life through schools, colleges, and cultural programs. But it is still not fully used in mainstream education.

Why is it underused?
  • British rule made people look down on traditional Indian learning.
  • English-style schooling replaced the old Gurukul (teacher-student) system.
  • Many ancient texts were not properly recorded or researched.
  • It was not connected to modern science or government policies.
Q2Do you think ancient knowledge is useful for society today? Support your answer.

Yes, absolutely. Ancient Indian knowledge offers practical and tested solutions for many modern problems.

🌿 Health

Ayurveda uses plants and nature for healing — no side effects.

🧘 Well-being

Yoga improves both mental and physical fitness.

🏠 Architecture

Vāstu Śāstra promotes eco-friendly and balanced design.

🧭 Ethics

Vedanta guides us on how to live honestly and peacefully in society.

Q9"The oral tradition of ancient India required unique methods to represent knowledge." Comment.

This is correct. Since most knowledge was spoken, not written, special memory techniques were developed.

  • Chandas (Meter): Texts were written as poems with rhythm — easy to remember.
  • Repetition & chanting: Students repeated texts many times for accuracy.
  • Guru–Shishya tradition: Teacher taught student personally — no distortion over generations.
  • Mnemonics: Clever memory tricks and short phrases stored complex ideas.
ResultThese methods kept Vedic texts accurate for over 3,000 years — before printing was even invented!

Q1What do you understand by 'Veda'? Why is it called 'apuruseya'?

Veda comes from the word vid = "to know." It is a sacred collection of knowledge from ancient India.

Why apuruseya?"Apuruseya" means "not made by a person." The Vedas are believed to have been revealed by God to ancient sages (rishis) — not written by any human. So they are seen as divine and eternal.

Q5Salient features of Rigveda Samhita.
  • Oldest of all four Vedas.
  • Has 1028 hymns organized in 10 books (Mandalas).
  • Written in ancient Vedic Sanskrit.
  • Praises gods like Agni (fire), Indra (rain), Soma, and Surya (sun).
  • Introduces Rita — the idea of cosmic order and natural law.
  • Covers nature, society, ethics, and prayers.
  • Hymns were recited by the Hotri priest during rituals.

  • Protection chants and prayers against illness and evil.
  • Rituals for birth, marriage, and death.
  • Social insights and philosophical reflections.
  • More practical and connected to everyday life than the other Vedas.

Q13Why is Shiksha an important Vedanga? What role does it play in oral tradition?

Shiksha deals with correct pronunciation — tone, accent, and rhythm of each sound.

  • Keeps Vedic chants pure and undistorted over thousands of years.
  • Wrong pronunciation in rituals was believed to give wrong results.
  • Since texts were spoken (not written), correct sound = correct meaning.
  • Without Shiksha, the entire oral tradition would break down.

Q15What is 'Sulba-sutra'? What is its use?

Sulba-sutra = "Rope rules" — texts with geometrical instructions for building fire altars used in Yajnas.

  • Contains rules for drawing squares, rectangles, and triangles with exact areas.
  • Includes early knowledge of square roots and diagonal lengths.
  • The Baudhayana Sulba-sutra contains an early version of what we call the Pythagorean theorem — centuries before Pythagoras!

Q17Why did Vedic people need Jyotisha (Astronomy)?

Q6What do you understand by 'Purusha' and 'Prakriti'?
Purusha (Soul / Self)

Pure awareness. It doesn't change. It just watches. It is eternal and formless.

Prakriti (Nature / Matter)

Everything we see, think, and feel. It is always changing — mind, body, world are all Prakriti.

Liberation happens whenPurusha realizes: "I am not my body, thoughts, or emotions." That realization = freedom (moksha).

Q10Comment: "Charvaka school has taken a different approach compared to other philosophical systems."

Yes, completely different. Charvaka is India's ancient materialist school — it rejects everything spiritual.

  • Believes only what you can directly see or experience is real.
  • Rejects: soul, rebirth, God, liberation — all are considered myths.
  • Says: live well, enjoy life, seek physical pleasure.
  • Does not accept the Vedas as valid.
ContrastEvery other school (Vedic or non-Vedic) believes in some form of spiritual growth. Charvaka alone is purely materialistic.
Q11One-page note on the Jaina school of philosophy.
  • Founded by Mahavira (~600 BCE). Main virtue = Ahimsa (non-violence) — including toward animals and insects.
  • Soul (Jiva) is eternal and separate from matter (Ajiva).
  • Liberation = removing all karma through right knowledge, right faith, and right conduct.
  • No creator God — every soul can achieve liberation on its own.
  • Teaches Anekantavada = "Truth has many sides" — respect all viewpoints.
  • Promotes vegetarianism, fasting, and non-possessiveness.

Linguistics
Q1Briefly sketch the organisation of Astadhyayi. How many rules does it have?

The Astadhyayi is the world's first complete grammar book, written by Panini around 500 BCE for Sanskrit.

Astadhyayi
▼
8 Chapters
Each chapter: 4 sections
Total: 32 sections
~4000 Sutras (rules)
  • Rules are written in very short, compressed Sanskrit — like code.
  • Arranged logically so each rule builds on the previous ones.
Q2What are the unique aspects of Sanskrit grammar propounded by Panini?
  • Rule-based and precise — every word follows strict rules, no exceptions without reason.
  • Meta-rules (Paribhashas) — rules about how to apply other rules.
  • Markers (Anubandhas) — tiny helper codes that indicate how a word changes.
  • Algorithmic — the grammar works like a computer program; input a root, follow rules, get the output word.
  • Compact mnemonics — entire groups of sounds stored in tiny syllables (Pratyaharas).
Q3How is phonetics addressed in Sanskrit? What are the phonetical aspects for vowels?

Sanskrit sounds are classified by where in the mouth the sound is made and how it is made.

Q4What do you understand by 'Prakriti' and 'Pratyaya'? Why are they relevant in Sanskrit grammar?
Prakriti (Root/Base)

The base word — like a verb root (dhatu) or noun stem. It carries the core meaning.
Example: "gam" = to go

Pratyaya (Suffix)

A suffix added to the base to change or refine its meaning.
Example: gam + a = gama (going)

TogetherPrakriti + Pratyaya = A complete Sanskrit word. All word formation in Sanskrit works on this simple but powerful principle.
Q5Short notes: (a) Sanskrit is derivational (b) Grammar is rule-driven (c) Grammar is modular.
FeatureSimple Explanation
DerivationalNew words are built by adding suffixes to roots. From one root, you can create hundreds of related words.
Rule-drivenEvery transformation follows a fixed rule (sutra). No guessing — it's like a mathematical formula for language.
ModularRules are grouped in independent modules. You can use one part without breaking another — like Lego bricks.
Q6How are noun forms (subanta) and verb forms (tinanta) generated in Sanskrit?
Noun Forms (Subanta)

Add Sup-pratyaya (nominal suffix) to a noun stem.
Depends on: gender, number (singular/plural), and case (who/what/where).

Verb Forms (Tinanta)

Add Tin-pratyaya (verbal suffix) to a verb root (dhatu).
Depends on: tense (past/present), mood, number, and person (I/you/he).

Q8What is a mnemonic? How is it used in Astadhyayi?

A mnemonic is a memory trick — a short phrase or code that helps you remember a long list of things.

  • Panini used the Shiva Sutra (14 lines) to encode all Sanskrit sounds in a compact sequence.
  • The Pratyahara technique = picking the first and last sound of a group to represent all sounds in between. e.g., "ac" = all vowels.
  • This makes referencing complex sound groups fast and efficient.

Q11Why is knowledge of Dhatus (verb roots) in Sanskrit very valuable?
  • Dhatus are the building blocks of Sanskrit vocabulary — all words come from roots.
  • One dhatu can generate hundreds of words by adding different suffixes.
  • Knowing roots = understanding any new Sanskrit word automatically.
  • Essential for reading classical texts, chanting, and translation.
  • In computing, dhatu recognition allows machines to parse and analyze Sanskrit text efficiently.

Q13Comment: "Sanskrit has potential for use in NLP and AI applications."
  • Sanskrit's highly regular grammar — no exceptions — makes it ideal for computer processing.

Q1Key inferences about the Indian mathematical tradition based on historical evidence.
  • Indian maths developed on its own, going back to the Vedic era (2000+ BCE).
  • The Sulba-sutras show advanced knowledge of geometry and measurement.
  • Indians invented zero, the place value system, and the concept of infinity well before the West.
  • Maths was used in astronomy, architecture, poetry, and religious rituals — not just for trade.
  • Indian numerals spread to the whole world through Arab scholars.
Q2When was Zero discovered in India? What is its special significance?
When was Zero invented?Around the 5th century CE — early hints in the Bakhshali Manuscript, then formally by Brahmagupta (628 CE) who gave rules for arithmetic with zero.
Why is Zero special?
  • Place value: Without zero, you can't write 10, 100, or 1000 — the whole decimal system depends on it.
  • Mathematics: Enables algebra, calculus, and modern computing.
  • Philosophy: Represents the concept of "nothing" or "void" (Shunya) — a deep idea in Indian thought.

Q4Key characteristics of a place-value system. How is it advantageous?
What is place value?The value of a digit depends on its position. Example: In 345 — the 3 means 300, 4 means 40, 5 means 5.
  • Uses base-10 — moving one position left multiplies the value by 10.
  • Simplifies addition, subtraction, and multiplication greatly.
  • Large numbers can be written compactly.
  • Zero fills empty positions, making numbers unambiguous.
Q5What is the decimal system? Where did it originate and what are its advantages?

The decimal system is a base-10 number system using digits 0–9. It originated in India.

  • Can represent any number — whole or fraction — efficiently.
  • Used universally in science, accounting, engineering, and daily life.
  • Easy to learn and compute with.
  • The foundation of all modern mathematics and computing.
Q7Indian mathematics is seamlessly linked with literature and poetry. Comment with examples.
  • Bhaskara's Lilavati presents math problems as poetic riddles in Sanskrit verse.
  • Pingala's Chandas-shastra used binary combinations (short/long syllables) to analyze poetic meter — a form of combinatorics.
  • The Katapayadi system encoded numbers inside Sanskrit words and verses for easy memorization.
  • Aryabhata wrote his entire astronomy-math treatise in verse — precise science in poetic form.
Q8What are the uses of word or letter numeral systems?
  • Used to encode numbers inside words or sentences for easy memorization.
  • Bhuta-sankhya: Everyday objects or concepts represent numbers (e.g., eye = 2, fire = 3).
  • Katapayadi: Sanskrit consonants represent digits, allowing mathematical formulas to be hidden in poetry.
  • Made it possible to memorize large numbers without writing them down.

Q10Why is the Bhuta-sankhya system called so?

Bhuta = element/object · Sankhya = number

It is called Bhuta-sankhya because it uses physical objects and natural concepts to symbolize numbers.
